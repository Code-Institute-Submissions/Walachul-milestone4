HISTORY

The Alpin Roman Club was founded in Bucharest, on March 18, 1934, at the initiative of a group entitled "Alpine Grouping". Soul and initiator C.A.R. was the young lawyer Nicolae (Nae) Dimitriu, who wrote, on March 21, 1932, to his friend Radu Titeica the following: “And I would have great joy to know that the future roads will be made under the aegis of the Alpin-Club, whose realization will not I see that when we are grouped together. I would dare to think of a monthly tourist publication ... ”. Being few, eight (engineer Gheorghe Frim, lawyer Nicolae Dimitriu, students Nicolae Comanescu, Alexandru Beldie and Vasile Nicolau, Traian Belitoreanu, Gheorghe Georgescu and Ion Sincan), first had to affiliate with an older association, ADMIR, in March 1933 Here the Alpine Grouping began to implement its plans. He published the quarterly magazine the Alpine Bulletin, in whose pages appeared a series of articles with descriptions of routes, accompanied by sketches, and organized a cycle of graded collective trips, in which a large number of lovers of the mountain participated.

After a year of fruitful coexistence with the ADMIR-host association, the Alpine Group withdrew and on March 18, 1934 laid the foundation of the Alpin Roman Club, taking over the Alpin Bulletin magazine. The establishment of the Alpin Roman Club was a necessity of the time, in order to remove it. the Romanian mountaineering from the old beech forests - the paths - and pointing towards the steep, rocky walls and the winter climbs, activity especially requested by the youth of those times and appreciated by the youth of today. Also, because of the need for guidance, hosting and, why not, schooling in alpine climbing.

In 1934-1948, CAR, according to its statute, continued the gradual group trips with people from outside the club, to make known to the amateurs the prahovan steepness of the Bucegi, others in other massifs of the country or abroad, marked roads to facilitate access. in the mountains, he built the Costila Refuge, in 1938, and the Alpine Camino, in 1939, for the rest of the climbers at an affordable price, he bought in 1947 a space in Bucharest (Elisabeta Blvd. 15) for the Club's headquarters, until 1937 the Bulletin was printed. Alpin, and in 1938-1940 the Bulletin of the Alpin Roman Club, organized the first school of modern technique in alpine climbing, thus raising the degree of difficulty from I to V, with sixth grade passages according to the UIAA norms, made the badge of the Club.

The initiators of the Alpin Roman Club, old carolers of the mountain beauties, in their pilgrimages captured on the photographic film wonderful images that illustrated the club magazine, the fruit of a special and disinterested work, which required years of physical training and photographic specialty. Each article in the magazines - representing pioneering work - some accompanied by sketches by Radu Titeica, Alexandru Beldie or Nae Dimitriu, contains references to the tracks, either for research or description.

The first climbing routes, using a rudimentary technique, were initiated and carried out by Nicu Comanescu: the northern edge of the Tower of Seciului, the Central Horn in the Corner of Malin and the Tip of Peak, all in Bucegi. Other young people followed, as was naturally the case: Niculae Baticu, Ion Trandafir and Dan Popescu climbed the Furcilor route in the wall of Galbenelilor, considered today by the 4th degree. From 1938 the modern climbing technique was introduced, new types of ropes and new espadrilles, beneficiary being. first of all, Toma Boerescu with the Great Surplomba from the yellow wall. After the war, in 1946, the routes of the Three Surplombe and the Crack of the White Valley Peak, by Niculae Baticu and Emilian Cristea, were made, and Sorin Tulea and Gh. Nicolescu participated in the first. At the same time, Ion Coman from Brasov has made a number of 11 premieres in the Bucegi and Piatra Craiului mountains, of which we mention the most important: Central Cracks in the White Valley wall, the Coman Trail in the Yellowstone wall, the oblique route in the Malaiesti Tower.

This fruitful activity was interrupted by the samavolic act of the communist authorities, which on June 11, 1948 dissolved the tourism-mountaineering associations, whose heritage was passed to the National Tourist Office. It should be noted that Romania was the only country in the Soviet bloc that dissolved the tourism-mountaineering groups. (History written by Niculae Baticu in 1990).

By the initiative of a rare man, Niculae Baticu, C.A.R. it was reborn in 1990, initially gathering under its name or several hundred members. A handful of people investing in their free time and making significant material donations have kept C.A.R. in the family of full members of the International Union of Mountaineering Associations (UIAA). Their objective was and is to revitalize C.A.R. for the benefit of all mountain lovers.
